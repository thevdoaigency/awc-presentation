Laystrom Hardware

The independent alternative to the roll-up.

Laystrom sells doors, frames, and hardware into a $9.5–14B commercial market that a private-equity consolidator has already started buying up in its own backyard. The strategy isn't to out-scale the roll-up. It's to be the thing the roll-up structurally can't be.

01The Market

A steady market, growing for the right reason.

The US commercial doors-and-hardware market sits around $9.5B and is expected to grow to roughly $14.2B by 2033 — a 5.2% CAGR driven by commercial construction, renovation cycles, and code-driven replacement (fire rating, ADA, security). Laystrom already sells into the sectors carrying that growth: education, healthcare, multi-family, municipal, hospitality, senior living, and public safety. This isn't a market Laystrom needs to find. It's a market it's already standing in.

The risk isn't demand. It's who captures it — a fragmented distributor market with a well-capitalized consolidator now buying share, sector by sector, city by city.

02The Competitive Landscape

The real competitor isn't another supplier. It's a platform.

In 2019, The Cook & Boardman Group — the nation's largest commercial door, frame, and hardware distributor — acquired American Building Services (ABS), a Chicago-area DFH distributor, and folded it into a 20,000+ SKU e-commerce catalog with two-day shipping and centralized systems integration. That consolidation wave hasn't slowed: as recently as this spring, Foundation Building Materials acquired Unified Door and Hardware, a 31-location distributor, in the same play. Laystrom isn't competing with a mom-and-pop shop anymore in its own market — it's competing with a national roll-up that already has a foothold in Chicago.

03Where Laystrom Stands

SWOT, grounded in what's actually true today.

Strengths

  • 73-year legacy in the Chicago market (est. 1951) — older than most of the roll-up's local brands
  • Genuinely full-category range: hollow metal, wood, aluminum, Division 10, acoustical, lead-lined
  • Estimating team with 20–30 years of experience — the credibility a spec-heavy sale runs on
  • New ownership since 2023 already pushing modernized software for submittals and estimates

Weaknesses

  • No visible e-commerce or online product catalog — Cook & Boardman ships from 20,000+ SKUs online
  • Thin digital footprint: no case studies, no project proof, no visible sector-specific content
  • Site reads as informational, not as a selling or spec-support tool for architects and GCs
  • No public trust signals beyond a single testimonial quote on the site

Opportunities

  • Position as "the independent" precisely while a consolidator is buying up the category — timing is on Laystrom's side
  • Municipal & public safety sector overlap with Leopardo Efficiency's city relationships — a warm cross-sell path inside the AWC portfolio
  • Spec-support content (fire ratings, lead times, line cards) is an open SEO lane nobody local owns
  • Careers page shows active hiring — a growing team is a growth story worth telling publicly

Threats

  • Cook & Boardman/ABS already has a Chicago branch, national backing, and e-commerce scale
  • The DFH distribution layer is an active PE roll-up target nationally (Unified/FBM, HC Quality Doors, BryComm — all bought in the last 18 months)
  • Manufacturer-direct accounts for large GCs can bypass distributors entirely on big projects
  • Being invisible online means being invisible to architects specifying products before a GC ever calls

05The Growth Trajectory

What Laystrom could grow into, once it leaves the nest.

Not a thought-leadership brand in the Curtis/Leopardo Efficiency sense — a regional opening-solutions authority. The company GCs, architects, facility managers, schools, and hospitals trust when a commercial opening needs to be specified, sourced, installed, maintained, and upgraded. Here's who already runs that playbook, at three different scales.

The Growth Line
Laystrom grows from "the hardware supplier Leopardo uses" into the regional authority on commercial openings, access, and hardware systems.
Worth naming directly: Cook & Boardman is both the biggest competitive threat in Section 02 and the best aspirational model here. That's not a contradiction — it's the whole strategy. Compete against their local branch on relationships and speed. Study their national playbook for where "opening solutions" eventually goes.
Best Aspirational Comp

Cook & Boardman / CBX Solutions

The same company that bought Laystrom's Chicago competitor in 2019 is also the clearest picture of the category's ceiling — "complete opening solutions" combining doors, frames, hardware, security, network technology, specification support, project management, installation, and maintenance. This is the direction to study, not the company to copy line for line.

Full opening-solutions platform: doors, hardware, security, systems integration
Nearer-Term Distributor Comp

Banner Solutions

A closer match to Laystrom's actual B2B reality — a PE-backed national wholesaler (High Road Capital Partners) of door hardware and electronic access control products, built on reliable logistics and specialized technical expertise, with a strict no-end-user-sales, pure-distributor model. Proof that staying a wholesale distributor and still building real category authority isn't a contradiction.

National wholesale distributor, door hardware + electronic access control
Top-of-Category Reference

Allegion

Not a company Laystrom becomes — a picture of where the category itself is heading. Allegion frames its business as "security around the doorway and adjacent area," spanning mechanical locks to advanced electronic access control and biometric systems. It's the proof that "the opening" is a strategic building system, not just a commodity part.

25+ brands, global door security & access control leader

Banner Solutions is the realistic near-term shape: stay a distributor, get sharper about logistics and technical expertise. Cook & Boardman is the mid-term shape: opening solutions as a full category, not just supply. Allegion is the ceiling: access control and security as the real value, hardware as the delivery mechanism.

06The Recommended Strategy

Don't out-scale the roll-up. Out-know it, on camera.

Cook & Boardman wins on catalog breadth and shipping speed. Laystrom can't beat that with a bigger warehouse — it can only beat it by making the estimating team's expertise visible before a buyer ever calls. That's a production and outreach problem as much as a strategy problem, so here's exactly what VDO Aigency builds to make it real: the video content, the outreach motion, and the AI stack behind both.

The Core Insight
A national e-commerce catalog can ship faster. It can't put 20–30 years of estimating judgment on camera. That's the one asset Laystrom has that Cook & Boardman structurally can't copy — so every deliverable below exists to make it visible.
07Video Content

Five formats built from what the team already knows.

Format 01

Spec-Support Explainer Series

Short, direct-to-camera or narrated walkthroughs on fire ratings, lead times, and install/spec guidance — the exact questions an architect or GC would otherwise call Laystrom to ask. Publish the answer before the call.

Built with: Voiceover Studio + Shot Builder cutaways
Format 02

Line Card & Product Spotlights

One short video per major manufacturer line — hollow metal, wood, aluminum doors, hardware categories — turning static line cards into something a specifier actually watches instead of skims.

Built with: Semantic Bridge (line card text → script)
Format 03

Jobsite Problem/Solution Shorts

Real field scenarios — a fire door replacement, a Division 10 install — framed as the problem Laystrom solved. This is the content type that actually shows expertise instead of claiming it.

Built with: Cutdown tool, sourced from longer estimator interviews
Format 04

The "Modern Distributor" Brand Film

One flagship piece built around Chuck Thomas's own framing — the shift from old-school manufacturer to technically savvy distributor since the 2023 acquisition. This is the anchor case for everything else.

Built with: Voiceover Studio narration + on-site footage
Format 05

Common Mistakes + Access-Control-Ready Hardware

A "what GCs get wrong" series (wrong fire rating, wrong hardware for the door type) paired with content on access-control-ready hardware — the first step toward the Allegion-style electronic security lane, without Laystrom needing to sell it directly yet.

Built with: Voiceover Studio + Shot Builder diagrams
08Outreach

Every inbound quote request becomes a trigger for a short, relevant spec-support video sent directly to that prospect — turning a static PDF quote into a live touchpoint that reinforces the expertise pitch.

Model: Email/LinkedIn sequence tied to quote-request data
Outreach 04

Sector-Targeted Content Drops

Municipal and education-sector content gets routed specifically toward AWC's existing warm relationships — the Leopardo Efficiency municipal book is a real distribution channel, not just a metaphor.

Cross-sell: Leopardo Efficiency's municipal contact base
09The AI Production Stack

Which tool does what, specifically for Laystrom.

Semantic BridgeRailway · /board-export
Turns line cards and spec sheets into video scripts automatically. Laystrom's product data is already structured — manufacturer, category, fire rating, finish. Semantic Bridge converts that structure into a first-draft script for each line card spotlight, cutting script time to near zero.
Voiceover StudioRailway · 5 voice IDs, ElevenLabs v3
Gives the spec-support series a consistent, credible narrator voice across dozens of short explainer videos — without needing an estimator on camera for every single one. Reserve real on-camera time for the flagship brand film and jobsite stories.
Shot BuilderRunway Gen-4.5
Generates cutaway diagrams for product anatomy — a fire-rated door assembly, a hollow metal frame cross-section — the kind of visual that makes a spec explainer actually teach something, without needing a physical product shoot for every SKU.
Cutdown ToolRailway
Turns every long-form explainer into LinkedIn-length spotlights — a 90-second line card video becomes three 15-second cuts, each targeted at a different buyer (architect, GC, facility manager).
