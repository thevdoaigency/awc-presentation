Leopardo Construction

We see buildings. We don't see evidence of building the buildings.

Leopardo is the anchor brand of the AWC portfolio — a nearly 50-year, family-founded contractor with the scale, safety record, and project roster to prove it. But its public channels show two stories: polished finished spaces and internal culture. The third and most commercially valuable one — the expertise that actually gets hard buildings built — is almost entirely missing.

01The Market

A large, fragmented market where expertise — not scale alone — is the differentiator.

The US commercial building construction market is large and recovering — roughly $560.5B in 2024, projected to reach $843.9B by 2032 — but it is deeply fragmented: the top 50 firms account for less than 10% of total revenue. The category is being reshaped by a persistent skilled-labor shortage, material-cost volatility, and private-equity roll-ups targeting specialized trades. In a market this crowded, the contractors pulling ahead are the ones who can visibly prove they control complex work — because rework and schedule slippage, not price, are what actually sink projects.

The fastest-growing backlogs are exactly Leopardo's sweet spots: mission-critical and data-center work, healthcare, and education. Turner alone reported a $12.6B data-center backlog heading into 2025. Leopardo is already the trusted builder for dozens of hospitals and has a deep municipal, aviation, and interiors portfolio — but a buyer scanning its public channels today would struggle to see any of that operational depth. The demand is concentrating around proof of expertise, and Leopardo's proof is sitting off-camera.

02A Finding Worth Fixing First

Two channels, one blind spot: the work itself is missing.

This isn't a credibility problem — Leopardo has the history, people, and projects. It's a content-mix problem with real commercial weight, visible in two places. On LinkedIn, the feed is monopolized by HR and recruiting content — employee celebrations, team outings, culture posts. Legitimate, but it has become the company's entire public voice by default. There's no visible project proof, no executive POV, no field expertise competing for space. On the website, the imagery is almost entirely completed-project photography — architectural and interior beauty shots. There's very little of the actual work: no rebar, no crews mid-pour, no trades on site, no process. For a company whose positioning is trust, scale, and execution, the site reads more like an interior-design portfolio than a builder's proof of capability.

The fix: don't cut the HR content — build the missing lane and let it compete for space. On LinkedIn that means renegotiating who owns the calendar and carving out a defined share for project proof and executive POV (see the recommended content mix in Section 05). On the website it means adding the missing middle — process, method, and the difficult decisions — to every project story. The raw material starts getting captured this week: President & CEO Giancarlo Pacini sits for an on-camera interview July 16, alongside live process footage from an active Chicago jobsite.
03The Competitive Landscape

Leopardo has the scale. What it isn't claiming is the authority.

Chicago is one of the most competitive contractor markets in the country — Walsh, Pepper, Power, Clune, JE Dunn and dozens more compete on relationships and reputation. Two family-and-management-owned Chicago peers, Pepper and Power, have made construction expertise a central, visible part of their public identity. Leopardo operates at comparable scale and depth — but sits low on the axis that increasingly decides work: how visibly a contractor proves it controls complex construction.

The open move is vertical, not horizontal. Leopardo doesn't need to get bigger — it's already operating in the top-right neighborhood on scale. It needs to climb the expertise-visibility axis into the quadrant Pepper already occupies: a relationship-driven, family-founded builder whose public channels prove, week after week, that it knows how to control the hardest work in the room. Everything Leopardo needs to make that climb already exists on its jobsites. None of it is on camera yet.

06The Growth Trajectory

What Leopardo grows into, once the expertise becomes visible.

The move isn't "more projects." It's the move from respected regional builder to a nationally legible authority — a contractor whose expertise wins work before the bid, because the market can see how it thinks, solves, and controls the hardest jobs. Three Chicago peers and one national giant have already run versions of this arc, and none of them stopped being relationship-driven to do it.

The Growth Line
Leopardo grows from "polished Chicago contractor with great finished projects" into the visibly authoritative builder for complex, mission-critical, and occupied environments — the one whose field expertise is its most public asset, not its most hidden one.

07The Recommended Strategy

Show the work behind the work — starting with footage captured this week.

Leopardo doesn't need a new story, and it doesn't need to invent footage. It needs to document the story already happening every day on its jobsites, and give its field leaders a public voice as experts — not just as culture participants. The pilot starts with the July 16 Chicago shoot: Giancarlo Pacini's executive interview, paired with live in-progress capture, and cut against the existing footage of Senior PM Sean Scott on-site at The Ave. Here's what VDO Aigency builds from it, how it's distributed, and which AI tools do the work.

The Core Insight
Leopardo currently displays the product of construction but rarely dramatizes the act of it. The finished building is visible; the judgment, coordination, and problem-solving that created it are not. The strategy isn't to write a new brand — it's to build the production and distribution system that turns invisible field knowledge into visible market authority.
08Video Content

Five recurring franchises, seeded from one shoot.

Format 01

Executive POV — The Giancarlo Interview

The anchor asset. President & CEO Giancarlo Pacini on camera July 16, speaking to scale, standards, and how Leopardo runs complex jobs. Cut against Sean Scott's existing "it's family owned, it's got that family feel" line from The Ave, this becomes a two-voice (President + PM) piece with no reshoot — and the top of every other franchise below.

Built with: July 16 interview + Cutdown tool
Format 02

The Leopardo Field Report

30–90-second recurring updates from active jobsites — a superintendent or PM on one real milestone, decision, or coordination challenge, shot in the real environment. Not generic progress reporting: the point is to show judgment and control. The lowest-lift, fastest-proof franchise, and it cuts directly from the Chicago process footage.

Built with: On-site field footage + Cutdown tool
Format 03

One Problem, One Solution

Case-based short-form: the problem → why it mattered → what the team decided → how it was executed → what risk, delay, or cost it avoided. This is the franchise that converts construction knowledge into market authority — turning a restricted-access site or a phasing challenge into visible proof of expertise.

Built with: Semantic Bridge (interview transcript → case study script)
Format 04

Built While Open

The ownable authority series: construction inside operating environments — infection control, phasing, occupant safety, business continuity. Leopardo's healthcare depth (40+ hospitals) makes this a differentiated lane competitors can't easily claim. Positions Leopardo as the builder you trust when the building can't close.

Built with: Semantic Bridge + Voiceover Studio
Format 05

The Superintendent's View

Direct-to-camera expertise prompts — "What's the earliest sign a schedule is in trouble?" "What do you check before approving a milestone?" This is what converts experienced field leaders from culture-post subjects into visible market experts, and doubles as recruiting content that speaks to the labor shortage head-on.

Built with: On-camera field footage + Cutdown tool
09Outreach & Distribution

Getting the work in front of owners, developers, and recruits.

Channel 01

The Renegotiated LinkedIn Calendar

The core distribution fix. Identify who owns the calendar today, then carve out a defined share for project proof and executive POV against a recommended mix — roughly 40% work-in-progress, 25% finished outcomes, 20% expertise, 15% culture. The HR content stays; it just stops being the whole channel.

Recommended mix: 40 / 25 / 20 / 15 across the four content lanes
Channel 02

Project-Page Rebuild

Every case study restructured around the missing middle: finished hero → the challenge → the constraint → active-construction imagery → the team member who solved it → the method → the measurable outcome. Turns the website from an interiors portfolio into a builder's proof of capability.

Distribution: leopardo.com project pages, proposals, and BD decks
Channel 03

AWC Portfolio Cross-Sell

As the anchor brand, Leopardo's executive and process content sets the visual and editorial standard for the rest of the portfolio — and shared shoots (Grand Kahn, Enlyt, DFH) amortize production across companies. Internal distribution no outbound campaign can replicate.

Channel: shared AWC portfolio production + BD relationships
Channel 04

Recruiting & Trade Channels

The Superintendent's View and Field Report cuts distributed as recruiting content through trade and apprenticeship channels — addressing the labor shortage as a distribution opportunity, not just an HR problem, using the same expertise story that attracts clients.

Channel: trade associations, apprenticeship programs, careers page
