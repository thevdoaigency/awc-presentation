00 / The Story We're Telling

What We're Going For

The Brief
We are telling the story of people who build real things. The work should feel proud, skilled, and human. Not slick. Not staged. Real.

Every site has three things worth bringing home. If you walk away with all three, you did the job.

01 / The Day Before

Before You Go

Pack This

  • Camera body and one backup battery (two if you can)
  • Two memory cards. Format both before you leave.
  • One wide lens, one zoom. That's enough.
  • ND filters. Sites are bright and open.
  • Monopod or small tripod. A full tripod is hard to move on a site.
  • Lens cloth and a blower. There will be dust.
  • Water and snacks. You will be out there longer than you think.

Wear This

  • Long pants. No shorts.
  • Closed-toe boots. Steel-toe if you have them.
  • Long sleeves are smart. Lots of sharp things.
  • The site will give you a hard hat, vest, and safety glasses. Wear them the whole time.
You cannot step onto the site without a hard hat, safety glasses, and a hi-vis vest. No exceptions. If the site does not give you one, do not walk on.
02 / Arrival

When You Get to the Site

  1. Park where the site tells you to park. Not in front of any gates. Not in any lane where trucks need to turn.
  2. Find the superintendent. They are the boss of that site. Do not start shooting until you have talked to them.
  3. Sign in if they have a sign-in sheet.
  4. Ask three questions: Where can I walk? Where can I not walk? Who is watching out for me?
  5. Take the safety briefing seriously. If they offer a walk-through, take it. That's also when you scout your shots.
  6. Keep your phone on. The super might need to reach you if something changes.
03 / On the Site

Stay Alive

A working construction site is the most dangerous place you will work this year. Treat it that way and you will be fine.

  • Look up before you stop walking. Stuff falls.
  • Never stand inside the swing radius of a crane or excavator. If the arm can reach you, you are too close.
  • Never walk behind a vehicle that's backing up. Drivers can't see you.
  • If someone yells, freeze. Then look around. Don't run until you know which way is safe.
  • Don't touch anything. Not tools, not materials, not switches. Not even for a shot.
  • If you don't know if something is safe, ask. The answer is always free.
If a worker tells you to move, move first. Ask why later.
04 / The Shot List

The Eight Shots

Try to bring back all eight from every site. Mix lenses. Mix angles. If you nail these, we have what we need.

01 / THE WORKER
35mm · EYE LEVEL

The Worker

Frame them from the chest up. Focus on the face or the hands. Let them keep working — don't pose them.

Eye level. We are not looking down on the worker, and we are not making them a hero. They are a person.

02 / THE SCALE
24mm · WIDE

The Scale Shot

Pull back. Make the site huge. Put one or two humans in the frame as scale markers — they make the building feel as big as it really is.

The structure should win. The people should look small.

03 / THE POWER ANGLE
20mm · LOW

The Power Angle

Get the camera low. Almost on the ground. Point straight up at a beam, a crane, or a worker on a ladder. Use the sky as your background.

This shot makes things look heroic. Use it once or twice — not for everything.

04 / THE DETAIL
50mm · MACRO CLOSE

The Detail

Get in close. Hands working a tool. Steel meeting concrete. The grain of wood. Wires going into a panel. Texture is the story.

Don't be shy. The closer the better. Use the longest lens you have or get physically near.

05 / THE LIGHT
35mm · BACKLIT

The Light

Find where the sun comes through. Workers in silhouette against bright light look strong. Dust, sparks, and steam all read as story — chase them.

Golden hour (first and last hour of daylight) is best. But you can find good light any time if you look for it.

06 / THE MOTION
35mm · 60fps

The Motion

Crane swinging. Excavator turning. Truck rolling in. Either move with it (pan) or hold steady while it moves through your frame.

Shoot motion at 60fps so we can slow it down in the edit. Looks twice as cinematic.

07 / THE WIDE
16mm · ESTABLISHING

The Wide Establishing

Show the whole site at once. Get high if you can — an upper floor, a hill, a parking structure across the street. Drone if it's been cleared.

Hold this one for 8 to 10 seconds. We need room to put titles or voiceover on top of it.

08 / THE SKY FRAME
35mm · NEGATIVE SPACE

The Sky Frame

Structure or crane against open sky. Use lots of empty space. Put the subject on one of the grid lines, not the middle.

This is where titles and quotes will land in the edit. Leave room.

05 / Camera Settings

The Cheat Sheet

Same settings on every site unless something forces a change. When in doubt, default to these.

Resolution
4K minimum. UHD or DCI both fine.
Frame Rate
24fps for normal shots. 60fps for anything moving you want to slow down.
Shutter
1/50 at 24fps. 1/120 at 60fps. (Double the frame rate.)
White Balance
5600K outside. Custom or match the lights inside. Never auto.
ISO
Base ISO. Bump up only when you have to.
ND Filter
Use one on bright days so you can keep the shutter at 1/50.
Picture Profile
Flat / Log profile. We will color in post.
Audio
Any interview subject or follow footage where the subject is narrating must be recorded with a body-worn mic — DJI Mic, wireless lav pack, or TASCAM DR-10L. Camera mic is only acceptable for silent B-roll.
Orientation
Shoot horizontal native. But compose every frame safe for 9:16 and 4:5 crops — keep the action and subject inside the center two-thirds so we can repurpose for social and mobile cuts.
Padding
Roll 5 seconds before the action starts. Hold 5 seconds after it ends.
Delivery
All footage must be sent to the media manager via MASV or another provided cloud system. Footage left on a Google Drive will not be counted as delivered.
06 / The No List

Do Not Shoot

  • Faces of workers who say no. Always ask before you make a hero shot of someone. If they say no, walk away. Wide shots where they're small are fine.
  • Anyone breaking a safety rule. If you see something that looks unsafe, lower the camera. We can't use it and it can hurt the client.
  • Client paperwork, plans, or blueprints. If a document is in the frame, move or change the angle. Same for laptop or phone screens.
  • Competitor logos or trucks. If another company's vehicle or sign is in the shot, reframe.
  • Anywhere you don't have permission to be. Locked areas, occupied floors, neighboring lots. The super tells you where you can be. Trust them.
  • Identifiable kids or members of the public. If a passerby is in your shot, blur or reframe.
07 / End of Day

Before You Leave

  1. Find the superintendent. Say thank you. Tell them when they will see the footage.
  2. Note any shot you want to come back for. Send the media manager a quick note so it doesn't get lost.
  3. Back up both cards before you leave the parking lot. Two copies. One stays with you, one goes to the cloud or a drive.
  4. Charge batteries that night. Format cards in the morning.
Never wipe a card until the footage is in two places. Ever.
