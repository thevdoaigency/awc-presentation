02 · State of the Brand Today

A real software product with quantified ROI, presented as a stock-image flyer.

Page 02

DFH Connect is AWC Technology's first commercial software product. It compresses two-to-four hours of manual data entry per project into under five minutes by bridging Emullion estimating software and QuickBooks Enterprise. That is a 95+ percent productivity gain — the rarest kind of marketing asset and the easiest one to sell.

The current product flyer leads with “Revolutionize Your Workflow” over stock imagery of an “AI woman” chatting with technology. None of the imagery matches what the product actually does. None of the copy names the audience that benefits. The productivity number — the single most powerful sales tool DFH Connect has — is buried under a feature grid weighted equally with “State-of-the-Art Technology.”

What works.

Quantified Outcome
Two-to-four hours per project compressed to under five minutes. A defensible, repeatable, sales-ready productivity claim. Most SaaS products in adjacent categories cannot make this kind of statement.
Clear Integration Target
Emullion estimating to QuickBooks Enterprise accounting. Two specific systems used by a specific buyer (door, frame, hardware distributors). Narrow targeting is a strength, not a weakness, for a first SaaS product.
Inside-the-Trade Provenance
Built by AWC Technology — the same group that operates door, frame, and hardware distribution at scale through Laystrom. Software built by operators who already run the workflow is a defensible competitive moat.

What does not.

Marketing Mismatch
A real productivity software is sold with a single-page PDF flyer featuring stock photography of AI assistants and unlocking smartphones. None of the imagery represents the product. The flyer signals "made in PowerPoint" to buyers who would otherwise sign a five-figure annual contract.
No Web Presence
The dfh-connect.com site appears to be an extension of the flyer rather than a real product page. No demo video. No customer logo. No pricing context. No founder story. Just a Sales@ email address.
Buried Lineage
DFH Connect is the first product of AWC Technology, LLC — the technology arm of a family office with decades of operating credibility. That parent identity is mentioned in the flyer body copy and nowhere else. The lineage is the moat. The marketing buries it.

04 · Who Benefits

Three audiences, each measuring the win in their own language.

Page 04

The current flyer addresses all three at once with a single feature grid. The right marketing speaks to each one in the language they use to justify the purchase to their CFO.

Accounting Teams

Same-day invoicing.

The cash flow argument

Invoices generate the moment the job ships, not three days later when manual entry catches up. Cash moves on the timeline the business operates on. A controller can justify the contract in one sentence to a CFO: faster cash, fewer errors, no overtime to catch up at month-end close.

Sales & Inventory

Orders match estimates.

The accuracy argument

Sales orders are built from the source data, not transcribed by hand. Line items match. SKUs match. New inventory items added automatically when the job calls for them. No more reconciliation calls between sales and the warehouse. No more shipping errors traced back to a typo in QuickBooks.

Project Management

Hours back, every job.

The capacity argument

The two-to-four hours per project that disappeared into manual entry return to the schedule. Across a year of projects, that is the equivalent of a full headcount reclaimed — without hiring, without adding overhead. PMs get to manage projects again instead of typing them.

05 · The Brand Gap

A product worth thousands per year, priced to the buyer like it costs a hundred.

Page 05

The marketing is teaching buyers to underpay for the value the product actually delivers.

Vertical SaaS in the construction trades typically prices between $200 and $800 per user per month for products that deliver this level of workflow compression. DFH Connect's marketing presents the product like a $99 utility download. The visual signals (stock photography, single-page flyer, Sales@ inbox) tell buyers what to expect to pay before they see a number. The longer the marketing teaches the wrong price, the harder it becomes to charge the right one.

What the product is worth
A door, frame, and hardware distributor running 200 projects per year saves 400-800 hours through DFH Connect. At fully-loaded labor cost, that is $25,000-$60,000 of annual productivity. A subscription priced at 10-15% of that value is reasonable. At 1-2%, it is unprofitable to serve.
What the marketing signals
A single-page flyer, stock imagery, no demo, no customer references, no parent company visible. The buyer reads this and prices the product at the floor of the category, not the ceiling.
What the buyer compares against
Without context, DFH Connect looks like a utility — one of dozens of QuickBooks integrations sold for under $50/month. The comparison set is wrong because the marketing established it.
The compounding cost
Every customer onboarded at the wrong price anchors the next sale. Re-pricing later is harder than pricing correctly now. The brand work is also the pricing work.
06 · Recommendations

Six moves over the first 90 days.

Page 06

The first software product of AWC Technology deserves the marketing of a software product, not the marketing of a service flyer.

01

Replace the flyer with a real product page.

A landing page that leads with the productivity number, shows the actual workflow diagram, names the audience, and offers a real demo. Built once, hosted at dfh-connect.com, indexed by Google, designed to support enterprise sales conversations. The first deliverable of the engagement.

02

Produce one cinematic case study.

Identify the strongest existing DFH Connect customer — presumably Laystrom or a comparable distributor — and produce a 60-90 second film. Real footage of the back office before and after. Named controller, named PM, real numbers. This becomes the proof asset that justifies the price.

03

Build the AWC Technology parent identity.

DFH Connect is product one of AWC Technology, LLC. GATOR is product two. There will be a product three. Establish the parent identity now — consistent typography, shared design system, common positioning — so future products inherit credibility instead of building it from scratch each time.

04

Re-anchor pricing through positioning.

Marketing and pricing are one decision in vertical SaaS. The brand work signals which competitive set DFH Connect belongs in — QuickBooks utilities ($50/month) or operational software ($500+/month). The recommendation is the latter, supported by everything else on this list.

05

Introduce a face to the brand.

SaaS in regulated, niche trades is a trust business. The technical founder or product lead becomes the public voice — LinkedIn presence, occasional speaking engagements, content that demonstrates depth of the workflow understanding. Buyers in vertical SaaS research the team, not just the features.

06

Adopt the AWC portfolio identity system.

DFH Connect currently looks unrelated to the rest of the portfolio. Aligning with the shared AWC visual system creates immediate trust transfer from Leopardo Construction, Laystrom Hardware, and the family office’s decades of operating credibility — while preserving DFH Connect's distinct product identity.

07 · Proposed Positioning
Software built by the people who already run the workflow.
A line that names the moat: DFH Connect isn’t SaaS that observed the door and hardware trade from the outside. It is software built by AWC Technology — the same group operating distribution at scale through Laystrom. Works on the homepage, in a sales deck, and at the top of every proposal.
